Project Editor

The project editor is where you will be making your project. When you first make a new project, you will see something like:


On the right is a workspace. Down the center of the editor, there will be some colored blocks. You will be able to see other blocks by clicking on the buttons above it, labelled "Looks", "Sounds", "Pen", etc. You can drag any of these blocks to form a code that will play when you press the green flag (next to the title of the project, on the top left).


All of the blocks are pretty self-explanatory. For example, the "move (10) steps" block will make whatever is selected (aka. "sprite") move 10 steps forward in whichever way it is pointing in. The "set x to (0)" block will simply set the sprite's x value to 0. However, some of the other blocks can be more confusing and take more work. But we'll come back to that later.


At the top of the blocks area, there are different tabs, labelled "Scripts", "Costumes", and "Sounds". If you click on "Costumes", Scratch's paint editor will show up. Here, you can upload an image to be on your sprite ,draw your own, or click "Add" at the top of the paint editor and choose from Scratch's library of sprites. When drawing your own, there are two modes: Bitmap and Vector. Bitmap is stored in pixels/squares, while Vector is stored in shapes. On the "Sounds" tab, You will see what music you have kept in that sprite. Different sprites and the backdrops can contain music that can be edited and played. Before any sounds are uploaded (or chosen from the Scratch sound library), there is already the default "pop" sound there. At the top of the audio visualizer (what shows the loudness in the song/sound), there will be the name of the sound/song and undo/redo buttons.


At the bottom of the visualizer, there is something labelled "Edit" and another one labelled "Effects". Using these, you can edit the sound, such as deleting a part of the audio or adding an effect to it, like making the volume softer. Next to the buttons, you can play the sound, stop playing the sound, or record yourself using Scratch. Note that when you record, the entire audio you record on will be wiped out, however, this can be undone. When you navigate back to the "Scripts" tab, the blocks and the workspace will reappear.


Now to the more complicated blocks.

Many people often get confused when it comes to the "Variable" blocks. When using Scratch, any round-oval-ish block is a variable. Variables are very useful when it comes to making complicated projects. They can store information, like numbers or some words. You can show the variables on your project stage by clicking the little check box. When you double click them while they're showing on the stage, they will change the way they look, but the value of the variable will not change. Only the look - this means it will not affect the code!
